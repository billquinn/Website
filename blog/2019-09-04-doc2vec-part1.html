Doc2vec.

Part 1.

In this first post, I want to share a tool I've been using to explore literary influence: doc2vec. I've been using doc2vec to look at how poetry gradually evolves in Poetry, a Magazine of Verse (and if doc2vec is capable of registering poetry's mutations). Poetry continues today, and was an important literary hub of modernism in the early twentieth century.

Doc2vec builds off its predecessor and namesake, word2vec, so starting there will explain why doc2vec offers one way to model literary change (and, potentially, a form of literary influence). Word2vec is a computational process designed to capture the semantic relationships between words. For thorough explanations of word2vec, I still refer to Benjamin Schmidt's Word Embeddings for the digital humanities, Lynn Cherny's Visualizing Word Embeddings in Pride and Prejudice, and The Women Writers Vector Toolkit. Word2vec aims to reconstruct the semantic relationships of words. To take one common example, the words king, queen, man, and woman share certain, culturally determined meanings that convey gender and class. That is, a word2vec model can predict cultural meaning with mathematical formulas, such as king - man = queen. By capturing the relationship between words, word2vec improves on other methods because it can recreate polysemy and understands analogies.

Image from Gidi Shperber's A Gentle Introduction to Doc2vec

In capturing semantics, word2vec also captures cultural biases embedded within any corpus, which Nissim, van Noord, and van der Goot discuss in Fair is Better than Sensational: Man is to Doctor as Woman is to Doctor. Although all algorithms are infused with bias, word2vec differs from other computational approaches which would only know king as a four-character sting of text. Schmidt argues that word embedding models, like word2vec, merit attention because they allow a much richer exploration of the vocabularies or discursive spaces implied by massive collections of texts than most other [computational] reductions out there. Applying word2vec to a corpus enables a large-scale computational reading that is more attuned to cultural meanings than most computational models.

Word2vec, therefore, is useful for studying poetic language that relies on nuanced combinations of words to create meaning. While word2vec transforms a corpus into a single, long string of text, doc2vec remembers the borders of documents, which can be set as issues of a magazine, individual poems, novels, or other documents. Doc2vec takes the relationships of words that word2vec discovers and re-packages them within their original textual boundaries. Doc2vec and word2vec propose a specific theory of language: semantics emerge from context. Each word maintains its many meanings; each meaning can be called forth as a vector that points toward other words that share the same semantic space. A single word’s multiple meanings can be highlighted or dismissed by querying in different accompanying words. For these reasons, Quoc Le and Tomas Mikilov, the developers of word2vec and doc2vec, report that doc2vec improves on previous methods used to recover cultural semantics and maintaining the order of words (2014).

Doc2vec’s logic of semantics maps onto figurative language. Similar words in concentrated textual spaces orients mathematical vectors as if turning their signifiers towards the same direction. In this way, I believe doc2vec can harmonize with figurative language, which, in a sense, also relocates the meaning of a text outside the literalness of words’ definitions (denotations) but within context clues (connotations). By leveraging the encoding practices of the Modernist Journals Project (MJP), doc2vec can start the process of sorting language-users into different communities.

Figure 2 refracts the different magazines into sensible clusters. The High Modernist magazines, such as The Little Review, Poetry, and Dora Marsden’s magazines (The (New) Freewoman and The Egoist) cohere on the right side of the graph. These magazines published canonical authors, like James Joyce, Amy Lowell, T.S. Eliot, and H.D. While these magazines on the right promoted avant-garde, experimental literature, the magazines on the left were more explicitly political. The Crisis was (and continues to be) the newsletter of the NAACP and was instrumental in fostering the Harlem Renaissance along with other magazines. Under its first editor, W.E.B Du Bois, the magazine published reports of racial prejudice and uplift alongside their literary sections. The Masses was a socialist monthly and was dubbed the most dangerous magazine in America (MJP).

Figure 2, then, reconstructs the old facade of High Modernism. The avant-garde magazines on the right, separated from the socially critical magazines on the left, recover their apolitical posturing. Scholarship has now thoroughly debunked the myth that canonized modernist were supposedly disinterested in politics and ianti-commercial. The Little Review, for instance, began as a socialist-anarchist magazine. Even after its notorious Blank Issue, where it committed to being a magazine of Art, the editors continued to grapple with readers who demanded a return to its socialist (political) roots.

While doc2vec reproduces this fossilized portrayal of modernism, this brief revival might actually signal that the computational process has worked well. The illusion of an apolitical modernism held a strong grasp on modernist studies until (more or less) the New Modernist Studies, when the field expanded beyond the canon, and the rise of periodical studies, which brought new materials into focus. Both movements complicated the history that many modernists ostensibly promoted (while I’m most familiar with these two subfields, I am sure there are others that also helped this shift). When doc2vec clusters these magazines into modernist and non-modernist camps, the results are not suggesting now-overturned myths of High Modernist were in fact accurate. Instead, I interpret doc2vecs results as attending to a shallower surface of language; doc2vec takes the modernist apoliticizing at face-value. As I hope to show in the next post, this doesn’t produce a roadblock and can actually be leveraged for more insightful readings.

Figure 3: Scatter Plot of Genres in MJP. Parsed by genre.

Figure 3 visualizes clusters of genres. Again, doc2vec seems to be sorting documents (the chunks of text encoded by the MJP) into discernable groupings. The visualization above reflects the MJP’s encoding practices, which markup genres within an issue as a bundle. So, all the poems in a single issue are bound together and, when they are parsed for computation, they appear as if a single string. Although the encoding is not as precise as a human reader, the markup does not prevent documents from clustering intuitively. The five main genres (poetry, ficiton, articles, letters to the editors, and advertisements) settle into their own localized sites. Articles and letters share the same space, which is a finding I am exploring further with my dissertation.

What I find promising about doc2vec’s clustering is that it reproduces expected intuitions about literary history, namely different magazines and genres use language differently and, therefore, occupy different semantic spaces. Doc2vec can leverage this encoding and move to another level of textuality, like peeling off a layer of an onion, and begin to model the ways genres inflect each other over time.

Figure 4: Chord Diagram of Genre Influence/Transfer

Using the same data, but filtered and computed slightly differently, we can see how doc2vec understands how semantics drift across genres over time. While the cluster graphs of Figure 2 and 3 show a static picture of textual distance, Figure 4 adds a temporal dimension by asking what genres are most like genres from previous years. This graph starts to visualize how genres converge.

Figure 4 shows the four main genres in Poetry: poetry, letters, articles, and advertisements. The ribbons that span across the interior of the circle indicate semantic similarity. The graph shows the trajectories of similarities amongst each genre. The poetry ribbon, for example, curls towards the letters arc, suggesting a semantic drift that moved from poems to the language of readers. This one-directional movement likely highlights the readers who discussed poetry through quoting, paraphrasing, or possibly adopting the linguistic patterns of the poems they read. A reader, perhaps, intentionally or unintentionally mimicked the writing styles of poems that she was reading and wrote down a similar cluster of words in her own letter. Readers discussing poems in a magazine for poetry is not significant. And yet, the graph elucidates the flow of semantics within the magazine. The modes of cultural production and the consolidation of literary taste manifests here as a ribbon unfurling from one arc to another. Although language modulates differently in each genre and within each magazine, doc2vec surfaces the hidden layer of semantics that run beneath.

I understand the doc2vec results above as both tentative and inviting. By reproducing some basic, even if outdated, expectations of modernist magazines, doc2vec seems to be capturing an aspect of magazine culture that discerns variations in magazine and genre. Much more testing is needed (with different datasets and different variations of the MJP corpus), but it works well for a general overview.

In my next post, I plan to continue using doc2vec to trace literary likeness between lines of poetry.
